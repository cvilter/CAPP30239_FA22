Exploration of Washington Post Police Violence Data

This page explores Washington Post data on the more than 1,500 people killed by police in the U.S. in 2015. In particular, it explores the data on victims' mental illness status from multiple angles.


Proportion of cases reported to involve mental illness

An important reference point is the overall proportion of officer-involved deaths in the dataset which were reported to involve mental illness ("Mental_illness" = True). A simple way to convey the values of a binary variable like this one is through a horizontal bar. We see that the majority of the cases represented in our 2015 dataset were not flagged as involving mental illness, but a substantial minority of 21% were.

Considering that our dataset is a national aggregation offered by the Washington Post, it's likely to be a cleaned-up version of a significant number of smaller, different data sources from across the country. To better understand how the Washington Post's "Mental_illness" flag captures the complicated topic of mental illness, it would be useful to know what data sources fed into the larger dataset, how each source handled the issue, and what decision rules were used to create the final flag.

Trends in proportion of cases involving mental illness over 2015

Keeping in mind the overall average provided in our first chart, 21%, it's interesting to ask whether there were any trends in the proportion of police killings reported to involve a mentally ill victim over the course of 2015. A line chart is a logical choice to represent this data because it allows us to easily understand movement in a variable of interest over time.

Our line chart shows a wide range - from 12 to 31% - and a cluster of noticeably higher proportions from March to June compared to lower rates in the second half of the year, July to December.

This chart would have more impact if it were combined with data from the surrounding years. Expanding our chart to contain multiple cycles of data - or comparing line charts for multiple years - would enable us to observe whether the cycle that appeared in 2015 constitutes an annual trend or an anomaly.



Manner of death by mental illness status

One way to dig deeper into the role of mental illness in police killings is to examine whether individuals with a mental illness are markedly different than others across other variables of interest. Here we compare manner of death between those victims whose cases were flagged as involving mental illness and those that were not. We see that individuals with a mental illness were notably less likely to be shot and more likely to be tasered. Exact frequencies are provided for context.

Given that we're comparing a categorical variable with few categories (shot, tasered, both) across a simple binary (no mental illness / mental illness), side-by-side donut charts effectively depict broad differences. Given additional categories, this type of visualization would be difficult to interpret.
